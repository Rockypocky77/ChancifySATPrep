Questions 7–12: Social Science Passage

Remote Work, Real Tradeoffs

In surveys, workers often praise remote work for flexibility, while managers worry about collaboration and productivity. The reality is less simple: remote work reshapes how work gets done rather than uniformly helping or harming it.

A recent study asked managers to rate productivity changes after their companies adopted remote or hybrid policies. The results showed that for fully in-office teams, 25% of managers reported productivity gains; for hybrid teams, 40% reported gains; and for fully remote teams, 45% reported gains. At the same time, managers of fully remote teams reported the highest concerns about communication breakdowns and employee isolation.

Remote work especially benefits tasks requiring long, uninterrupted focus. Programmers and writers, for example, often report fewer distractions at home than in open-plan offices. But complex projects demanding constant coordination can suffer when every quick question becomes a scheduled video call.

Remote arrangements also change when work happens. Asynchronous communication—messages answered hours later rather than immediately—allows people in different time zones to collaborate. Yet it can also blur boundaries between work and home, leading some employees to feel "always on."

The debate over remote work often pits productivity against well-being, as if companies must choose one. A more useful question is: under what conditions does remote work support both? Clear expectations, thoughtfully scheduled synchronous meetings, and explicit norms about response times can help.

Remote work is neither a panacea nor a disaster. It is a tool. Like any tool, its value depends on how deliberately we use it.

Questions 13–18: Natural Science Passage

Coral Reefs at the Edge

Coral reefs occupy less than 1% of the ocean floor yet support an estimated 25% of marine species. Corals themselves are animals, but they rely on a partnership with microscopic algae living inside their tissues. The algae perform photosynthesis, providing corals with energy; in return, corals offer shelter and nutrients. This symbiosis builds the colorful, limestone structures divers admire.

When ocean temperatures rise even a few degrees above normal for extended periods, this partnership breaks down. Stressed corals expel their algae, turning ghostly white—a phenomenon called bleaching. Bleached corals are not immediately dead, but without their algae they are starved and vulnerable. If temperatures drop quickly enough, algae can return. If stressful conditions persist, the corals die.

Scientists describe reef survival as a race against reaching a thermal "threshold." A global analysis of satellite data showed that, in the 1980s, major bleaching events occurred roughly once every 25–30 years in a given reef region. By the 2010s, some regions experienced severe bleaching every 5–6 years, leaving little time for recovery.

Local actions—reducing pollution, managing fishing, and establishing marine protected areas—help reefs cope with warming waters. Healthier reefs bounce back faster after heat waves. Still, there are limits to resilience. No amount of local management can permanently protect reefs in steadily warming oceans.

Coral reefs thus serve as both victims and warning systems. Their decline reveals how tightly biological systems are tied to temperature. Saving reefs will require not only careful local stewardship but also global reductions in greenhouse gas emissions.

Questions 19–24: Humanities Passage

Libraries as "Third Places"

Urban planners sometimes describe healthy communities as having "third places"—locations that are neither home (the first place) nor work or school (the second place), but shared spaces where people gather. Parks, cafes, and community centers can serve this role. Increasingly, so can public libraries.

For decades, libraries were viewed primarily as quiet book repositories. While that function remains important, many modern libraries have expanded far beyond it. A single branch might offer language classes, tax-preparation help, job-search workshops, and free internet access. Children build robots in makerspaces; teens record podcasts in small media studios; older adults learn to navigate smartphones.

These services matter most for people who lack resources elsewhere. In neighborhoods where residents cannot afford high-speed internet, a library computer may be the only way to complete homework or job applications. For recent immigrants, a library can be both classroom and social hub. In that sense, libraries are not luxuries but infrastructure—like roads or power lines—for democratic participation.

Critics sometimes complain that libraries have become too noisy or cluttered, straying from their original mission. But this criticism assumes that silence is the only sign of learning. A story time full of wiggling toddlers or a crowded resume workshop may look chaotic, yet both represent communities investing in their own futures.

In an era of rising inequality and digital fragmentation, public libraries remain one of the few truly accessible civic spaces. Whether patrons come for books, broadband, or simply a safe place to sit, libraries function as third places where different lives intersect—and where the idea of a shared public still has room to breathe.

Questions 25–30: Paired Passages – Homework

Passage 1

Homework, when designed thoughtfully and assigned in reasonable amounts, reinforces learning. A brief set of practice problems can help students consolidate new math skills; reading assignments can prepare them for richer in-class discussions. Studies consistently find that moderate homework loads in high school—roughly an hour per night—correlate with higher achievement.

Importantly, homework should serve a clear purpose. Copying vocabulary definitions or filling out busywork worksheets teaches students to comply, not to think. But tasks that ask students to explain a concept in their own words or apply it to a real-world scenario deepen understanding. Over time, managing deadlines and planning out longer projects also builds responsibility and time-management skills.

The solution to homework stress is not abolishing homework altogether. It is assigning less of it, more carefully.

Passage 2

Homework advocates often treat it as a sacred tradition rather than a policy choice. Yet for many students, homework does more harm than good. Surveys of high-schoolers in competitive districts show teenagers regularly working three to four hours per night, reporting sleep deprivation, anxiety, and little time for family, friends, or exercise.

Supporters argue that homework builds discipline. But unstructured free time and extracurricular activities can build discipline, too—while also supporting mental health. A student who practices piano, plays on a soccer team, or works a part-time job is hardly learning less about responsibility than one who spends the evening filling in worksheets.

None of this means learning should stop at the classroom door. Instead of assigning heavy nightly workloads, schools could encourage independent reading, family projects, or voluntary enrichment activities. Homework should be a tool students use, not a burden they carry.
